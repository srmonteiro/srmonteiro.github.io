Romantic Roots

In the 1800s, many US colleges and universities were founded establishing
their own traditions and musical cultures. Romantic era pieces were used
as the melody for alma maters which reveals that they were a part of oral
traditions in the US and sung widely along with popular and religious tunes.

I thought the trend to have the melodies of alma maters borrowed from Romantic music was more widespread across the country from my biased experience in the Midwest. My alma mater is the University of Michigan so I was most familiar " The Yellow and Blue" (1886) based off of Michael William Balfe’s The Enchantress. I had also frequently heard neighboring Michigan State University’s "Shadows"> which resembles Donizetti’s  Lucia di Lammermoor  and Northwestern’s Hymn  (1907) based on Haydn’s  St. Anthony Chorale which Brahms wrote a variation. Another notable example is the University of Wisconsin’s  "Varsity"  from the tune of Gounod’  "Domine Salvum Fac"  from the St. Cecilia Mass, which was chosen by music professor Henry Dyke Sleeper to accompany lyrics he wrote. These midwestern schools likely had more contact due to being geographically close which could account for a similar practice in setting the alma mater to music from a classical genre.

When looking at the alma maters I examined, I found borrowing the melody from classical music of the Romantic era was the fourth most common practice.



This indicates that classical music was being circulated in university communities along with folk, popular, and religious music around the time alma maters were being written. Whereas, today, classical music is often reserved for the elite and for selected locations, like the concert hall or galas, classical music was in popular circulation with the middle class in the late 19th century in America. Most alma maters were written in the late 19th/early 20th century and passed on through oral traditions and through university musical organizations.

There are three main models for how alma maters came to be written 1. The Glee Club wanted a new song or began singing a song that was adopted as the alma mater 2. The students or administrators were inspired by another school’s alma mater 3. The University held a song contest.

Glee Clubs were advocates for school song repertoire. In many cases, glee club members would write alma maters because they wanted something to sing to represent their university community. The University of Virginia’s glee club president set words to “Auld Lang Syne” for their alma mater. This is the case with Indiana University one of the first schools after Cornell to set the alma mater to the tune of “Annie Lisle.” The Indiana University alma mater was written for the glee club to have a song to sing in a competition with other schools. This suggests that glee clubs were competing with one another from different universities.

Schools often describe a situation after a sporting event or other occasion in which students were directly inspired by another school to create their own alma mater. For example, after a football game in 1902, Ohio State students were reportedly envious of their rival school Michigan for their alma mater and glee club member Fred Cornell wrote the song on the train ride home. Schools were likely to copy features of other school’s alma maters that were geographically close or who encountered one another regularly at sporting events or glee club competitions.

The song contest model invited members of the university community and alumni to send in poetry to be chosen for the alma mater (often with a monetary reward). The poem was set to music, most likely already in existence, although sometimes later composed to fit the text. Notable examples include Princeton University’s “Old Nassau” which was an original composition, the University of Pennsylvania’s “Hail, Pennsylvania,” set to Russia’s “God Save the Tsar!,” and the University of Michigan’s “The Yellow and Blue” which will be covered more in depth on the next page.

The alma maters with Romantic melodies are some of the more successful ones because the music had fewer issues with harmony and voice leading than those originally composed by students. These melodies were also deemed appropriate for inspiring the right amount of nostalgia for university life. Although, the idea of using a Romantic music melody seems to have insularly circulated predominantly in the Midwest because these schools compared themselves through frequent contact. These schools copied the idea of what type of melody to use, however, the melody itself was not directly copied as with “Annie Lisle” for example. In addition, there is no trend of national derivation with classical melodies adopted from Italian, English, French, and Austro-Germanic traditions. Which shows a remarkable variety considering that, compared to the East Coast, the Midwest had a relatively new classical music tradition with the Detroit Opera House (1869), the Auditorium Theatre (1889), and Chicago Symphony Orchestra (1891). Still, classical music was popular as evidenced by the way these alma maters were circulated and accepted as long-standing traditions.

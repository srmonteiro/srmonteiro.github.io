The University of Michigan’s alma mater, “The Yellow and Blue,” exemplifies many of the characteristics of a typical alma mater. However, the song’s unique traits make for a useful test case for examining how alma maters came into exist and how the melodies circulated at the time.

English Professor and alumnus, Charles Mills Gayley, wrote the text for “The Yellow and Blue” winning a song contest put on by the university. The text was set to the Pirate’s Chorus called “Ever Be Happy” from Michael William Balfe’s The Enchantress (1845).

Today, the melody is more likely to be heard as the University Michigan’s alma mater rather than in any other context. Balfe’s work is rarely performed today, although, his English language operas were popular in the 19th century. Among Balfe’s work, The Enchantress is one of his least written about operas. William Tyldesley questions why all three of Balfe’s major biographers mysteriously leave out details about The Enchantress, only mentioning that it had been performed at Drury Lane starring Anna Thillon. After the London premiere, performances took place in New York, Philadelphia, and San Francisco.

Dublin born Michael William Balfe was one of the most popular composers of English-language opera. English language opera was popular in the US throughout the 1860s and 70s. Katherine K. Preston explores English-language opera companies that were prominent in the post-bellum South performing primarily European repertoire translated into English. In her book Opera for the People, she argues that despite popular belief that opera was reserved for social elites, at the time, people of what we would now call middle class were enjoying English language opera regularly. The use of the melody from The Enchantress in “The Yellow and Blue” attests to the opera’s popularity.

People were likely singing the chorus regularly around the time Gayley was writing the lyrics to “The Yellow and Blue” in 1886. In his own account of the song in a 1925 interview, Gayley explains how he chose “the Pirates’ Chorus from one of Balfe’s operas” which he regarded as “an inspiring and not too hackneyed air.” Gayley’s family was Irish so he may have been more familiar with Balfe’s work leading him to choose the melody. However, he doesn’t mention The Enchantress specifically indicating the opera’s relative obscurity in 1925 when he was recalling writing the song.

Gayley’s interview also tells us how the song became a part of the campus culture. He mentions the glee club promoted it widely and his lyrics were circulated by pamphlet on campus for about three years.

In addition to its use in “The Yellow and Blue,” the melody was also adapted to an American patriotic song “Raise the Bright Flag of Columbia” in 1849. Before the civil war, the melody was adapted to this patriotic version and sold as a printed quartet by Lee & Walker in Philadelphia. A copy of “Raise the Bright Flag of Columbia” has been digitized by the Library of Congress. It was likely being sold soon after The Enchantress was performed in Philadelphia. This version of the song complicates how the melody was being circulated. At the time, was the melody more closely associated with Balfe, the Irish, English-language opera composer? Or were people familiar with the tune from “Raise the Bright Flag of Columbia”? Whichever the case, Balfe’s melody was adapted into American culture and is still performed today most frequently as the University of Michigan’s alma mater, “The Yellow and Blue.”

The Yellow and Blue

Sing to the colors that float in the light;
Hurrah for the Yellow and Blue!
Yellow the stars as they ride through the night
And reel in a rollicking crew;
Yellow the field where ripens the grain
And yellow the moon on the harvest wain;
-Hail!
Hail to the colors that float in the light
Hurrah for the Yellow and Blue!


Blue are the billows that bow to the sun
When yellow-robed morning is due.
Blue are the curtains that evening has spun
The slumbers of Phoebus to woo;
Blue are the blossoms to memory dear
And blue is the sapphire and gleams like a tear;
-Hail!
Hail to the ribbons that nature has spun;
Hurrah for the Yellow and Blue!


Here's to the college whose colors we wear,
Here's to the hearts that are true!
Here's to the maid of the golden hair,
And eyes that are brimming with blue!
Garlands of bluebells and maize intertwine,
And hearts that are true and voices combine;
-Hail!
Hail to the college whose colors we wear;
Hurrah for the Yellow and Blue!

Conclusion

An effective alma mater has a familiar melody and evokes nostalgia for the college years. The tradition of alma maters has persisted at least in part as a way to fuel sentimental attachments to the university that lead to major gift donations. In an article on Harvard’s alma mater, “Fair Harvard,” the band director explains how the band acts as a “spirit unit” at alumni events and “encourages alumni to raise song”—not to mention give money—“to Harvard.” Alma maters are used as a way to engage alumni and raise funds. Princeton’s alma mater displays dedication to the university proclaiming “our hearts will give while we shall live.”

Roosevelt University hosted a contest to write an alma mater for the school. A text was chosen and a composition faculty member wrote the accompanying music. This is common practice for many alma maters around the late 19th early 20th century. However, for Roosevelt University, this process took place in 2003.

At events, alma maters are usually sung a cappella, or with a wind band. From personal experience, sometimes the only people who know the words to the alma mater are those involved with vocal organizations/glee clubs or the marching band at a University. Marching bands will often play or lead an alma mater and are keepers of that tradition. What is the role of the alma mater today? And who knows it anyway?

Most alma maters are printed on programs for event for widespread singing with just the lyrics in strophic form and not accompanying music printed. Even at church occasions or weddings, pamphlets with simple melodies are notated and distributed so that that people can pick up the tune. However rudimentary their musical ability, most folks can pick up high and low pitch or an indication of the rhythm from having printed music rather than just lyrics. Still in University communities, it would seem that event organizers make the assumption that attendees know (or can quickly pick up) the tune, but need a reminder of the words. Or perhaps it is a belief that the words should be printed because they resonate more specifically with the University and the music is superfluous. Either way, this appears to be the most common iteration of presenting the alma mater at formal events.

At informal events, the alma mater seems to usually be sung a cappella by a group of students. In these formats, it is almost always followed by the singing of the fight song.

Another reason the Roosevelt University alma mater may have been a flop is because of its large scoring. Garrop wrote the piece for full orchestra and SATB choir and scored the winds in two distinct parts. It seems that it was written for a specific occasion in which the school’s orchestra would be provided. With such a large score and a format that would be expensive to recreate externally, the piece does not have versatility. A piano reduction with single vocal melody, for example, would have allowed for more accessibility for the song to be distributed or learned across the University community. The words to the alma mater are printed for the audience without any musical notation. With a new alma mater, it seems that the audience would have a hard time signing along because they would still be unfamiliar with the tune.
